About

The Nan Myint Tower, also known as the Watch Tower of Inwa (Ava), is one of the most prominent historical landmarks remaining from the days when Inwa served as the royal capital of several Burmese kingdoms. Often referred to as the "Leaning Tower of Inwa," it stands as a dramatic reminder of the city’s once-glorious past. The tower was originally part of the royal palace complex during the reign of King Bagyidaw, who ruled from 1819 to 1837 during the Konbaung Dynasty. It was constructed in the early 19th century and served as a watch tower, likely used for keeping guard over the surrounding plains and for viewing royal processions or important events. At 27 meters (about 90 feet) tall and built with brick and mortar, the tower was designed to be both functional and majestic, towering above the flat terrain of the Inwa plain. Nan Myint Tower is especially known for its slight tilt, caused by damage sustained during the 1839 earthquake, which destroyed much of Inwa’s royal buildings. Despite the collapse of the palace itself, the tower survived—though visibly slanted—earning its reputation as Myanmar’s own version of the Leaning Tower. Its survival through centuries of natural disasters makes it a powerful symbol of resilience

Architectural Features

The architectural design of Nan Myint Tower is a striking example of early 19th-century Burmese royal architecture, blending both grandeur and functionality. Built during the reign of King Bagyidaw as part of the royal palace complex in Inwa (Ava), the tower rises approximately 27 meters (90 feet) high and was originally used as a watchtower for observing the surrounding landscape and overseeing royal activities. Constructed primarily from brick and mortar, the tower features a square base that tapers as it ascends, creating a sturdy yet elegant silhouette against the flat plains of Inwa. The structure once included interior stairways leading to upper levels, from which guards or royals could look out over the city and nearby rivers. Its decorative elements were simple yet stately, in line with the functional role it served within the palace grounds. After suffering damage in the devastating 1839 earthquake, the tower developed a visible tilt, which remains today and gives it the nickname "the Leaning Tower of Inwa." Despite this, the tower has stood the test of time, a resilient remnant of the once-mighty capital, and an enduring symbol of Myanmar's royal architectural legacy.

Geographical and Strategic importance

Nan Myint Tower’s geographic location and strategic importance are closely tied to its role within the royal city of Inwa, which was a prominent capital of several Burmese kingdoms. Situated on the flat plains near the confluence of the Irrawaddy and Myitnge rivers, the tower provided an excellent vantage point for overseeing vast stretches of surrounding land and waterways. This elevated position allowed guards and royal officials to monitor approaching visitors, potential threats, and royal processions, ensuring the security and smooth functioning of the palace complex. The tower’s height made it a crucial lookout post in an era when early warning of enemy movements or natural events could mean the difference between safety and disaster. Furthermore, its proximity to major river routes gave it strategic control over important transportation and communication channels. Today, the tower’s location still offers visitors sweeping views of the historic Inwa ruins and the peaceful landscape, reflecting its former role as a guardian of the royal capital.
